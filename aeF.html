Participating in a traditional barbecue on a rural estancia (ranch) to taste grass-fed Argentine beef.

When we do an Asado, we aren't just 'grilling.' It’s a slow-burn thermal process using Quebracho wood to infuse smoke without burning the fibers.

Gauchos had no refrigeration, so they cooked the meat immediately using the Asado con Cuero (cooking it inside the hide) to keep the moisture in.

The Chimichurri Emulsion Recipe:

Base:

1 cup Parsley (chopped), 4 cloves Garlic (minced), 2 tbsp Oregano, 1/2 cup Olive Oil, 1/4 cup Red Wine Vinegar, 1 tsp Chili Flakes.

The Secret:

Let it sit for 24 hours. The vinegar undergoes a maceration process, drawing the fat-soluble flavors out of the herbs into the oil.
